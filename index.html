Jóhann Jóhannsson

The Icelandic composer who reimagined orchestral music

Here's a time line of Jóhann Jóhannsson's life and career:

  • 1969 - Born in Reykjavík, Iceland
  • 1980 - Learns piano and trombone, though gives them up in his teenage years.
  • Late 1980's - Jóhann attends Reykjavík University to study language and literature. His music career begins when he works as a guitarist and producer for many Icelandic indie rock bands.
  • 2002 - Jóhann releases his first full-length studio album, Englabörn. Englabörn is Icelandic for "Angel Children"
  • 2006 - A fourth studio album, IBM 1401, A User's Manual, is released. THe album was inspired by his father who was one of Iceland's first computer programmers. He used his hardware to compose songs in his free time.
  • 2011 - Jóhann is praised for raising social and cultural awareness of an extinct industry, coal mining, with his composition for The Miners' Hymns. He worked alongside filmmaker Bill Morrison to produce the film and orchestral acoompaniment that brought remembrance to the solemn lives of British coal miners.
  • 2013 - Composes the score for film Prisoners, which he is most known for.
  • 2015 - His score for the film Sicario is nominated for Academy Award for Best Original Score.
  • 2015 - Wins the Golden Globe Award for Best Original Score for his work in the film The Theory of Everything.
  • 2018 - Jóhann is found deceased in his Berlin home. It was found that a combination of medication played a likely role in his death.
  • 2020 - His only self directed and scored film, Last and First Men premiers. It is an adaption of the novel written by Olaf Stapledon, which is a science fiction novel describing the history of humanity and their ultimate downfall. The movie was filmed in black and white.

“In his work there is a solemn melancholia. For me, at its deepest core, all Johann’s music is about one profound question: why does God not answer any of our phone calls? He was a true collaborator and I felt quickly, as I was working with him, that the act of creativity would not suffer from the dance between our egos. He was a creative brother to me.”

-- Filmmaker Denis Villeneuve
